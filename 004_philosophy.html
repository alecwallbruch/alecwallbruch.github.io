Why Philosophy?

and why I love it!

My friend Azad staring towards the ocean

Philosophy gets you thinking

I took my first philosophy class during my freshman year of college, and it was titled "The Meaning of Life." Pretty heavy topic if you ask me, but I welcomed the mental challenge ahead of me! The class made me confront ideas that I tried to avoid, such as why do people have morals? Why should we care for people other than ourselves? Or what happens after we die?

Those are loaded questions, but they are questions that make you dig down inside you and question what you believe in and why you do. Thinking about these things is a part of being human.

The peaceful peaks in Telluride, Colorado

Philosophy keeps me grounded

Depending on what one believes in, it's easy to get caught up in one's thoughts. The idea of death is always one that scares me the most to think about, especially because it's challenging to grasp the idea of forever. Taking philosophy courses has forced me to come face to face with many ideas that are similarly as difficult to think about, like is there really such thing as true objectivity? Or are we able to prove our existence?

The difficulty or ambiguity of these questions are not what keep me grounded, but instead that I have the ability to think about them at all. It is fascinating that someone, whether it be you or me, has the capacity to even start to answer these questions.

Beatiful clouds at sunset above Memorial Union

Philosophy makes you a better person

Surely that claim sounds outrageous, but to me, it's true. Simply taking a single philosophy course will not make you a better person. Passing that class is not the key to enlightenment. What does make you a better person though, is having a reason to learn new things and explore different viewpoints. I never thought I would take a class that asked me if I think someone has the right to die, but after being exposed to that idea, it led me down a path that helped me learn what can make people's lives better.

Being actively engaged in the world around you makes you a more experienced, understanding, and caring person. Regardless of what you think or feel, you have learned more about the world around you and that made you more human; and I've never heard anyone say being more human is a bad thing.
